Are You Killing Your Lawn by Cutting it Too Short?

May 29, 2024  ·  Lawn Mowing

At Sprout Lawn & Landscape, we love mowing lawns, but we know that for many people, it is not their favorite thing to do. A lot of homeowners mow their lawns as short as possible to avoid having to mow more frequently. However, this is not a good practice — cutting your lawn too short can end up damaging or even killing your grass.

Here's how you can irreparably harm your lawn in Noblesville, Carmel, Westfield, and nearby Indiana areas by cutting it too short during the growing season.

Why Does it Hurt Your Lawn to Cut it Too Short?

In order for your grass to effectively absorb nutrients, the blades have to be kept at a certain height. When they are too short, the grass is harmed by the heat of the sun and is exposed to too much sunlight. Additionally, the shorter grass allots more energy toward growing the blades instead of creating a deeper root system.

This weakens your grass, blades, roots, and the overall structure of the lawn. A weakened lawn will be much more susceptible to lawn diseases or insect infestations. It also will not be able to fight off weeds and can be overtaken by invasive plants.

Lawn disease, insects, and weeds all have the potential to quickly kill off your lawn, destroying the hard work you put in to cultivating it.

How Tall Should Your Grass Be During the Growing Season?

The prescribed height of grass blades varies by the type of grass you have in your lawn. In our area of Indiana, we primarily have cool-season grasses such as fescue and Kentucky bluegrass. During the growing season, they should be kept at certain heights:

  • For most cool-season grasses, keep the height between 3.5 and 4 inches
  • Kentucky Bluegrass does best at 2.5 to 3.5 inches
  • Tall Fescue thrives at 3 to 4 inches
  • Fine Fescue can be kept at 2.5 to 4 inches
  • Perennial Ryegrass performs well at 2.5 to 3.5 inches

The One-Third Rule

The best practice for maintaining grass height is the one-third rule: never cut more than one-third of the grass blade length in a single mowing session. This ensures that photosynthesis continues at maximum efficiency and the grass can recover quickly between cuts.

For example, if your target height is 3.5 inches, you should mow when the grass reaches about 5 inches. Cutting it from 5 inches down to 3.5 removes about one-third of the blade — the safe amount.
