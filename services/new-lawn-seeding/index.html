The Process

How We Seed a New Lawn

Soil prep is everything. The seed itself is the easy part.

Soil Preparation

This is where most DIY seeding projects fail. We grade and level the area, remove debris, break up compacted soil, and amend it if needed. For new construction properties, this often means bringing in topsoil because the existing surface is compacted fill dirt with almost no organic content. Good seed on bad soil grows bad grass.

Seed Selection

We use professional-grade seed blends selected for central Indiana's climate. Most mixes include a combination of Kentucky Bluegrass (for density and color), Perennial Ryegrass (for fast establishment), and Tall Fescue (for drought and heat tolerance). The exact blend depends on sun exposure, soil conditions, and how you use the lawn.

Application and Coverage

Seed is applied at the correct rate using calibrated spreaders for even coverage. We ensure good seed-to-soil contact, which is the single biggest factor in germination success. Light rolling or raking after application presses seed into the prepared surface.

Establishment Period

New grass needs consistent moisture for the first 3-4 weeks. We provide clear watering instructions and timeline guidance so you know exactly what to expect at each stage. Most lawns are ready for their first mow 6-8 weeks after seeding.

Seeding vs. Sod

Which One Is Right for You?

Choose seeding when: you have time to wait 6-8 weeks for establishment, you want the widest selection of grass varieties, budget is a factor (seeding costs significantly less than sod), and you're planting in early fall when conditions are ideal.

Choose sod when: you need an instant lawn, the area has erosion concerns that can't wait, you're planting during a less ideal window like late spring or summer, or the area is highly visible and needs to look finished immediately.

Both options produce a great lawn when done correctly. The difference is timeline and budget. We offer both services and can recommend the right approach during your free on-site estimate.

If your existing lawn just needs thickening rather than a complete restart, aeration and overseeding may be the better and more cost-effective option.
